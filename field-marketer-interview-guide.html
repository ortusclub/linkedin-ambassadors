Interviewer Guide

Field marketer interview

This interview is mostly you explaining what we do and seeing if they're comfortable + a good fit. Keep it a conversation, not a grilling.

Before you start — the goal

You're checking three things: (1) can they talk to people and represent us well, (2) are they reliable, and (3) are they comfortable with what we do once they understand it. Be warm and honest — if they're uneasy about the model, better to find out now.

1Open & put them at ease

Say something like"Hi [Name], thanks so much for hopping on! This is really casual — I mostly want to walk you through what the role is and what we do, and then hear your thoughts and any questions. Sound good?"

Then a couple of light warm-up questions to get them talking:

"Tell me a bit about yourself — what have you been up to lately?"
"Have you done any promo / on-the-ground / people-facing work before?"

2Explain what LinkedVelocity does

The simple version"So — think of LinkedIn as Facebook, but for professionals. In sales and marketing, it's the #1 place businesses find clients. The catch is LinkedIn limits how many people you can reach from one account. So businesses hit a wall — one account only grows them so far.

That's the gap we fill. We connect businesses that want to reach more people with everyday professionals who have a LinkedIn account they're barely using — the business gets to grow, and the account owner gets paid every month for it. The people who share their accounts are our ‘ambassadors.’"
Then the role"Your job would be to find those ambassadors — out on the ground handing out flyers, talking to people, and helping the interested ones sign up. That's really it."

3The pay (be upfront — it's a strength)

  • ₱2,000/day base, for every day you work — flexible / on-call.
  • +₱500 for every person who successfully signs up and gets onboarded — uncapped.
  • They can also boost it by sharing their own link online, not just flyers.
Frame it as"So there's a guaranteed daily rate, plus real upside — the more good sign-ups you bring in, the more you earn on top."

4The question they'll likely ask: "Is this against the rules?"

This will come up. Be honest — don't dodge it. Honesty here builds trust and weeds out people who'd be uncomfortable. The truth is: yes, account sharing is against LinkedIn's terms of service — but it's not illegal, and we do it responsibly. Here's how to answer:
Say something like"Good question, and I'll be straight with you. Technically, LinkedIn's terms don't allow account sharing — so it sits in a grey area, like a lot of growth and automation tools do. It's not illegal, and no one gets scammed. Here's why we do it responsibly:
  • It's always with the owner's consent. Every account is shared willingly by a real person who gets paid — nothing happens without them agreeing.
  • The renter never gets their password. We hold the login securely and set our email as the recovery contact (to handle any 2FA / security checks), but the renter only accesses the account through our software — they never see the login. The owner can change it or pull their account back anytime.
  • We screen who rents. Accounts go to vetted, legitimate businesses for normal outreach — not scammers, not anything harmful.
  • It's kept safe. Access runs through secure software (an anti-detect browser + dedicated proxy), so it behaves like normal usage.
  • Worst case is rare and recoverable — an account could get temporarily restricted, but that's uncommon with our setup, and the owner always chose to take part.
"So it's a grey area we're upfront about — but it's consent-based, screened, and secure. That's the whole model."
✅ Tone: calm and matter-of-fact. You're not defensive — you're just being transparent. If someone's clearly uncomfortable after this, that's useful info: they're probably not the right fit, and that's fine.

5Open the floor — their thoughts & questions

"That's basically it — what are your thoughts so far?"
"Any questions, or anything that feels unclear?"
"Is this something you'd feel comfortable talking to people about?"

What good answers sound like — green flags:

  • Curious and asks practical questions ("where would I hand out flyers?", "how do I get paid?").
  • Comfortable with the model once explained — not squeamish.
  • Sounds confident talking to strangers; maybe has done sales/promo/events before.
  • Reliable vibe — asks about schedule, takes it seriously.

Red flags:

  • Clearly uneasy or judgmental about the model even after the honest explanation.
  • Only interested in the money, no interest in the actual work.
  • Shy / uncomfortable talking to people (it's a people-facing role).
  • Flaky signals — vague on availability, low energy.

6Close & next steps

Wrap up"This was great, thanks [Name]! I've got what I need — we'll be in touch about next steps. In the meantime, if any questions pop up, just message me."

If they're clearly a strong yes, you can tell them there and then and cover: which areas they'd cover, how flyers + their sign-up link work, and payout timing. If you're unsure, keep it to "we'll be in touch" and decide after.

Quick cheat sheet
  • Open warm → light warm-up questions.
  • Explain LinkedVelocity (Facebook-for-pros → reach limit → we connect businesses + account owners).
  • Role + pay (₱2,000/day + ₱500/onboarding).
  • "Against the rules?" → honest: yes it's against LinkedIn's terms, but consent-based, screened, secure, not illegal.
  • Their thoughts + questions → read comfort + fit.
  • Close → next steps.
